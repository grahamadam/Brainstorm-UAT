# M03 · Where is this going?

**AI 101 · Module 3 of 7**
**Estimated time: 30 minutes content + 10 to 15 minute knowledge check + applied activity**
**Level transition: This module completes the literacy half of AI 101. By the end, you'll have your bearings on where the field is heading, which sets up the practical craft modules that follow.**

---

## Module brief

This is the trickiest module in the course, and it's worth being upfront about why. The first two modules dealt with what AI is and how we got here. Both of those have factual answers. This module is about where it's going, which doesn't. Predictions about AI have a long track record of being either wildly overoptimistic or wildly underestimated, and people in the field have been wrong in both directions plenty of times.

So this module isn't going to give you predictions. It's going to give you something more useful: a working framework for thinking about the trajectory honestly, the trends that are most likely to keep going, and the signals worth watching as the technology evolves. The goal is for you to leave this module with calibrated bearings, not a roadmap.

We'll lean heavily on what Anthropic and other credible sources have actually published, because those are arguments you can engage with rather than assertions to take on faith. Where we venture into interpretation, we'll mark it as such. You should feel free to disagree with anything in this module, because confident certainty about AI's future is itself a kind of red flag.

The honest stance for an L2 user in 2026 is: I have a working sense of where this is going, I'm watching specific signals to update my view, and I'm comfortable holding the uncertainty without it paralyzing me.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

1. Distinguish, in your own thinking, between what we can reasonably project from current trends and what's genuinely speculative.
2. Name the trends that are real and likely to continue, and why.
3. Articulate what's most likely to change for knowledge work over the next two to three years.
4. Build a personal practice of watching specific signals so you can stay oriented as the technology evolves.

## Lesson 1: How to think about AI predictions

Before we talk about what's coming, we need a way to think about *thinking about* what's coming. AI is a domain where confidence is cheap and accuracy is hard, and that combination produces a lot of bad predictions. The first move at L2 is recognizing this and developing a more disciplined way to engage with forecasts.

A useful frame: any claim about AI's future falls somewhere on a spectrum from "extrapolating from solid trends" to "pure speculation." Both ends of that spectrum exist in the public conversation, often presented with the same level of confidence, which is part of what makes the discourse so confusing.

Here's how the spectrum looks in practice:

**Reasonable extrapolation.** "Models will continue to get better at coding tasks over the next year." This is well-grounded. The trend is real, the data shows it's continuing, and the underlying drivers (scaling, better training, better tooling) are all progressing. You can plan around predictions like this.

**Plausible projection.** "Within two years, AI will be able to handle most routine knowledge work tasks at a quality level useful in professional contexts." This is reasonable but uncertain. It depends on continued capability gains, on adoption keeping pace, and on the integration challenges being solved. Not crazy, but not bankable.

**Speculation.** "AI will replace most knowledge workers within five years." This is in territory where confident claims are more about the speaker's worldview than about evidence. The honest answer to "how many knowledge workers will be replaced by AI in five years" is "we don't know, and the people who tell you they do know are usually selling something."

**Pure conjecture.** "AI will achieve consciousness within a decade." There's no rigorous basis for forecasts like this in either direction. They're worth ignoring, full stop.

When you read AI predictions in the wild, learn to silently sort them onto this spectrum. The further out a prediction goes, the higher the confidence interval around it should be. When you see someone making a five-year forecast with the same certainty as a six-month forecast, that's a signal that something is off in their epistemics.

This isn't about being skeptical for skepticism's sake. It's about being able to update at the right pace. People who hold confident predictions firmly tend to either ignore evidence that contradicts them or flip dramatically when reality outpaces their model. People who hold their views with appropriate uncertainty can update smoothly as new data arrives. The L2 move is the second one.

> **Try this (3 minutes).** Find a recent article about AI's future (search "AI predictions 2026" or similar). Read the first three predictions in it. For each one, silently sort it onto the spectrum above: reasonable extrapolation, plausible projection, speculation, or pure conjecture. Notice if the article's tone matches the actual epistemic strength of the claims. Most articles don't make this distinction, which is part of why the discourse is so noisy.

## Lesson 2: The trends that are real

With the epistemics in place, we can actually look at what's happening. There are at least four trends that are well-documented enough to project forward with reasonable confidence.

### Capability gains are continuing

The simplest and most important trend is that AI models are continuing to get more capable. Anthropic's CEO Dario Amodei has written that, behind the volatility of public sentiment, there has been a smooth and unyielding increase in cognitive capability since the field's modern era began. Every few months public discourse swings between "AI is hitting a wall" and "AI is about to change everything," but the underlying capability curve has been remarkably steady.

The practical implication is that the model you're using today is probably not the best one you'll use in six months. Capabilities that feel novel right now are likely to feel ordinary by the end of this year. This is why the principle-based learning philosophy in M01 matters: skills tied to specific tool capabilities will go stale, but skills tied to how to work with AI will keep paying off.

### Adoption is broadening, not narrowing

The other major trend is that AI is moving from niche tool to general infrastructure. Anthropic's Economic Index, which tracks usage patterns across the economy, shows that as of early 2026 about 49% of jobs have seen at least a quarter of their tasks performed using Claude. That figure has grown steadily across multiple reports. The data also shows usage diversifying. The top 10 tasks accounted for 19% of conversations in February 2026, down from 24% in November 2025. People are using AI for more things, not just more of the same things.

This pattern has direct implications. If 49% of jobs are seeing meaningful AI use today and the trend is broadening, it's reasonable to project that within 12 to 18 months, "uses AI in their work" will go from being notable to being the default among knowledge workers. That's not a wild prediction. It's an extrapolation of a trend that's already well underway.

### The user experience gap is widening

This is one of the most under-appreciated patterns in the data, and it matters specifically for you as a learner. Anthropic's research has found that more experienced AI users have measurably higher success rates than newcomers, and that the gap is showing up in observable behaviors. Experienced users iterate and refine; newcomers tend to use AI in a single-shot, directive way. Experienced users treat AI as a thinking partner; newcomers treat it as a vending machine.

The implication is that fluency itself is becoming an asset. The gap between people who know how to use these tools well and people who don't isn't theoretical. It's measurable, and it's growing. This is a major part of why Brainstorm exists. The L2 transition isn't just a personal milestone. It's the beginning of being on the right side of a widening gap.

### The work is shifting toward augmentation, not automation

There's a common assumption that AI's main effect on work will be automation: AI takes over a task, and the human is no longer needed for it. The data shows something more nuanced. As of early 2026, the majority of professional AI use is *augmentative*, meaning AI is collaborating with the user to produce better work, faster, rather than replacing the user entirely. This pattern has held across multiple Economic Index reports.

That doesn't mean automation isn't happening. It is, especially in narrow workflows that lend themselves to it (customer support routing, certain coding tasks, structured data processing). But the broader pattern is that AI is changing *how* people work more than it's changing *whether* people work. Anthropic's data shows that even where automation is increasing, the dominant mode of professional use remains augmentation.

This too has implications. If the dominant pattern is augmentation, then the people who win are the people who become genuinely good at working with AI as a partner. If the dominant pattern were full automation, the right strategy would be different. The data so far supports the augmentation framing, with the caveat that the pattern is still evolving.

> **Try this (2 minutes).** For each of the four trends above, ask yourself: *if this trend continues for the next two years, what changes for me in my work?* You don't have to write your answers down. Just notice which trends feel most relevant to your role and which feel more abstract. That's a useful starting point for the activity at the end of this module.

## Lesson 3: What's likely to change for knowledge work

Now we can be more specific about implications. Several shifts in knowledge work are already underway and likely to deepen over the next two to three years. Each is grounded in current data, with the explicit caveat that "likely" doesn't mean "certain."

**The baseline expectation will rise.** Tasks that took two hours and produced average work will take 30 minutes and produce above-average work, because AI handles the first draft. This isn't speculation. It's already happening for early adopters. As adoption broadens, what's "normal output" will shift, and the work that distinguishes you from a baseline performer will be different than it is today. Less about producing the deliverable, more about the judgment, context, and verification you bring to it.

**The role of expertise will shift but not disappear.** A common worry is that AI will make expertise obsolete. The data suggests something subtler. AI is currently most useful when paired with someone who has domain knowledge, because that person knows what to ask, how to evaluate the answer, and where the model is likely to be wrong. Expertise becomes the lens through which AI's output gets refined. In Anthropic's survey data, people in higher-skill jobs reported the largest productivity gains from AI, which is consistent with expertise being multiplicative with AI capability, not replaced by it.

**Some specific tasks will get automated.** Some tasks that were full jobs five years ago will be partially or mostly automated within the next two years. This is happening fastest in coding (where API workflows for routine code generation have grown rapidly), customer support (especially structured support workflows), and certain kinds of data analysis. Other tasks will resist automation longer because they require physical presence, complex relationships, or judgment that's hard to formalize.

**The learning curve gets steeper for newcomers.** This is the consequence of the experience gap from Lesson 2. As the people who've been using AI well for two or three years pull ahead, the difficulty of catching up grows. Someone starting from zero in 2027 will be facing a different competitive landscape than someone who started in 2024. The L2 transition is meaningful precisely because it puts you on the early side of this curve, even if you feel late to the party.

### What's not likely (caveats and counterweights)

A few things worth noting on the other side.

We are *not* on a smooth glide path. The AI Fluency Index research found that even as AI capability has grown, several behaviors that are critical for using it well (like questioning the model's reasoning or identifying missing context) have actually *decreased* in conversations where AI produces polished outputs. Capability and fluency aren't moving in lockstep. The technology is getting more powerful faster than the user base is getting more sophisticated. That gap has consequences and isn't automatically going to close.

We don't know what the medium-term economic shape will look like. There's healthy debate about whether AI will primarily augment knowledge workers (most people use AI to do their existing job better) or displace them (some jobs significantly contract or disappear). The current data favors augmentation, but the picture could shift. Anyone who tells you with confidence which way it'll land is overclaiming.

We don't know how much of the recent capability gains will continue at the same rate. Scaling laws have held for a long time, but every technology eventually hits diminishing returns. The current evidence suggests we're not there yet, but it's a real open question that the field is watching.

The honest stance is: the trends that are real are likely to keep going for at least the next year or two. Beyond that, uncertainty grows fast.

## Lesson 4: How to stay oriented

You can't predict where AI is going, but you can build a practice of watching specific signals so you update at the right pace. This is the practical takeaway of the module: a personal framework for staying calibrated as the technology evolves.

Here's how it works.

**Pick a few signals you'll watch.** Not 20. Three to five. The signals should be specific (not "AI capability") and ideally observable in ways that don't require expert interpretation. Examples of good signals: the latest Anthropic Economic Index report, a specific benchmark you'll check quarterly, a specific use case in your own work where you'll track whether the model is getting better, the rate of new tool integrations within Gong's stack.

**Decide in advance what would change your mind.** This is the underrated step. For each signal, write down what you'd expect to see if the trend is continuing and what you'd expect to see if it's breaking. That way, when new data arrives, you can update your view based on actual evidence rather than vibes. If you don't decide in advance, you'll rationalize whatever data comes in to fit your existing belief.

**Set a cadence.** Once a month, once a quarter, whatever fits. The point is to actually look at the signals on a schedule, rather than only when the discourse pulls your attention. Most of what you read about AI in the news is noise. Your own watch list is signal.

**Stay open to being wrong.** This is the discipline at the heart of all of it. The people who navigate technology shifts well aren't the ones who predict them most accurately. They're the ones who notice when they're wrong and adjust. This applies to optimists and skeptics equally. If the trend lines you're watching break, you should update toward the new picture, even if it contradicts what you previously thought.

The version of you that's most useful in 2027 isn't the one with the most confident view of where AI is going. It's the one with the most honest, regularly-updated view, who's watching specific things and adjusting as new evidence arrives.

## Key takeaways

- AI predictions live on a spectrum from reasonable extrapolation to pure conjecture. The further out a forecast goes, the higher the uncertainty should be. Be wary of confident long-term predictions.
- Four trends are well-documented enough to project forward: **capability gains are continuing**, **adoption is broadening**, **the experience gap between fluent and novice users is widening**, and **the dominant mode of professional use remains augmentation rather than full automation**.
- Likely shifts for knowledge work over the next two to three years: **the baseline expectation rises**, **expertise becomes more multiplicative with AI**, **some specific tasks will get automated**, and **the learning curve gets steeper for newcomers**.
- What's *not* likely: a smooth glide path. The technology is getting more capable faster than the user base is getting more sophisticated, and that gap has consequences.
- The L2 practice for staying oriented: pick a few specific signals, decide in advance what would change your mind, watch them on a regular cadence, and stay open to updating.
- Confident certainty about AI's future is itself a red flag. The best stance is calibrated bearings, not a roadmap.

## Knowledge Check

10 questions. Aim to answer in 10 to 15 minutes. Each question has one best answer; explanations follow each.

**1. Which of the following predictions is the most "reasonable extrapolation" rather than speculation?**
A) AI will achieve consciousness within a decade
B) AI models will continue to get better at coding tasks over the next year
C) AI will replace 80% of all knowledge workers within five years
D) AI will solve all major scientific problems by 2030

> **Correct: B.** Continued capability gains in well-measured domains like coding are well-grounded in current trend data. The other options vary from speculation (C) to pure conjecture (A, D). The further out a claim goes, and the more sweeping it is, the higher the uncertainty should be.

**2. According to Anthropic's Economic Index data, what's been happening to the diversity of tasks people use Claude for?**
A) Concentration is increasing; people are using AI for fewer kinds of tasks
B) Diversity is increasing; people are using AI for more kinds of tasks, with the top 10 tasks making up a smaller share of conversations over time
C) The pattern is flat; the same tasks dominate as a year ago
D) The data is too noisy to tell

> **Correct: B.** The top 10 tasks dropped from 24% of conversations in November 2025 to 19% in February 2026. People aren't just using AI more; they're using it for a wider range of things. This is one of the cleaner signals that AI is moving from niche tool to general infrastructure.

**3. Which statement most accurately describes how the user experience gap is evolving?**
A) Experienced and new AI users perform about the same on most tasks
B) New users are catching up quickly, so the gap is closing
C) Experienced users have measurably higher success rates than newcomers, and the gap is widening
D) The gap exists but isn't observable in real data

> **Correct: C.** This is one of the most important findings for you specifically as a learner. Fluency is becoming an asset, the gap is real and growing, and being on the right side of it is the practical reason for taking AI 101 seriously.

**4. As of early 2026, which is the dominant mode of professional AI use?**
A) Full automation, where AI takes over a task and the human is no longer involved
B) Augmentation, where AI collaborates with the user to do better work, faster
C) Random one-off use with no consistent pattern
D) Demonstrative use, mostly to impress others

> **Correct: B.** The data has consistently shown augmentation as the dominant pattern. Automation is happening in specific narrow workflows, but the broader pattern is AI changing *how* people work, not *whether* they work. This shapes what fluency looks like.

**5. Which of the following is *least* likely to be a productive way to think about AI's future?**
A) Watching specific, observable signals on a regular cadence
B) Deciding in advance what would change your mind about a particular trend
C) Reading every breathless headline and updating your view accordingly
D) Holding views with calibrated uncertainty rather than confident certainty

> **Correct: C.** Most AI discourse is noise. Reactive updating to whatever's loudest produces views that swing wildly without reflecting underlying changes. A, B, and D are all components of a disciplined practice for staying oriented.

**6. Based on the patterns in this module, what's most likely to happen to "the baseline expectation" for knowledge work output over the next 2-3 years?**
A) It will fall, because AI does work less well than humans
B) It will stay the same; AI is mostly hype
C) It will rise, because AI handles first drafts and what was "good" becomes "average"
D) It will become unmeasurable

> **Correct: C.** This is one of the most direct implications of broadening AI adoption. As AI handles the easy parts of more work, the bar for what counts as good work shifts. The work that distinguishes you will increasingly be the judgment and context you bring, not the deliverable itself.

**7. Anthropic's survey data found that people in higher-skill jobs reported what about their AI productivity gains?**
A) Lower productivity gains than lower-skill workers
B) Roughly equal productivity gains to lower-skill workers
C) The largest productivity gains, suggesting expertise is multiplicative with AI capability
D) No measurable productivity gains

> **Correct: C.** This is one of the strongest counterweights to the "AI replaces expertise" narrative. In the data, expertise paired with AI tends to outperform either expertise alone or AI alone. The implication is that becoming more skilled and becoming more AI-fluent compound rather than substitute.

**8. The AI Fluency Index found something important about the relationship between AI capability and user fluency. What was it?**
A) They move in perfect lockstep
B) Fluency is increasing faster than capability
C) Capability is increasing faster than fluency, and some critical fluency behaviors actually decrease when AI produces polished outputs
D) Both have plateaued

> **Correct: C.** This is the gap that creates risk. The technology is getting more powerful faster than users are getting more sophisticated, and in some specific cases (like when AI produces polished-looking artifacts) users actually become *less* discerning. This is part of why M05 on hallucinations matters so much.

**9. What's the right response to a confident long-term AI prediction in the news?**
A) Accept it if it comes from a credentialed source
B) Reject it on principle as hype
C) Sort it onto the spectrum from extrapolation to conjecture, and weight it by how far out it goes
D) Wait for someone to confirm it before deciding

> **Correct: C.** The L2 move isn't to accept or reject. It's to calibrate. Long-term predictions deserve appropriately wide uncertainty, regardless of who's making them. This is true of optimistic and pessimistic forecasts equally.

**10. The module's recommended practice for staying oriented as AI evolves involves which of the following?**
A) Reading every AI-related news article published
B) Picking three to five specific signals, deciding in advance what would change your mind, watching them on a regular cadence, and staying open to updating
C) Avoiding AI news entirely
D) Only listening to the loudest predictions

> **Correct: B.** This is the practical core of the lesson. A disciplined practice with a few specific signals beats noisy reactive updating, every time. The specificity and the pre-commitment about what would change your mind are what make it work.

## Applied Activity: "Three Things You're Watching"

**Estimated time: 25 to 30 minutes**
**Submission: a structured document with three signals + a short reflection**
**Graded against the rubric below**

### What you'll do

Build the start of your personal AI watch list. The point isn't to predict the future. It's to commit to specific signals you'll track, in a way that lets you update your view based on actual evidence as time passes.

You'll identify three signals. For each one, you'll specify what you're watching, why it matters, and (this is the key part) what specifically would change your mind about your current expectations.

### What good signals look like

Good signals are specific, observable, and tied to something that matters for *you* in your role. Generic signals like "AI capability" don't work because they're too broad to track meaningfully. Specific signals like "the next Anthropic Economic Index report's data on tasks in my field" or "whether Gong's call summary feature catches up to the level of detail I'd write myself by Q3" do work. Pick signals where you'll know it when you see it.

### Format for each signal

For each of your three signals, write a short structured entry:

**1. What I'm watching.** One sentence describing the signal as concretely as possible.

**2. Why it matters.** Two or three sentences on what this signal tells you, and why it's relevant to your work specifically.

**3. What I currently expect to see.** What's your honest current expectation over the next 6 to 12 months?

**4. What would change my mind.** This is the most important part. What would you need to see to revise your expectation? Be specific. "If I see X, I'll update toward Y."

### After the three signals

Add a short reflection (about 100 words) answering: **Which signal was hardest to make specific, and why?** The honest answer often reveals where your thinking is fuzzy, which is useful information.

### Submission

Submit as a single document. Each of the three signals should be clearly structured with the four sub-sections above. The reflection at the end can be informal.

### Rubric

You'll be graded on four dimensions, each on a 1 to 5 scale, for a composite out of 20.

| Dimension | 1: Minimal | 3: Solid | 5: Excellent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Specificity of signals** | Signals are vague ("AI gets better") or untestable | Signals are concrete enough to actually track over time | Signals are precise, observable, and tied to specific outcomes you'll genuinely notice |
| **Relevance to your work** | Signals are generic to the discourse, not tied to anything you actually do | At least one signal is clearly tied to your role; others are at least relevant | All three signals are clearly tied to specific aspects of your role or workflow |
| **"What would change my mind" criteria** | Vague or absent. No clear standard for updating | Each signal has a reasonable updating criterion, even if not airtight | Updating criteria are pre-committed and falsifiable; you'd genuinely update if reality went the other way |
| **Honest engagement with uncertainty** | Either over-confident (signals locked to a single prediction) or under-confident (everything is hedged into meaninglessness) | Reasonable balance: holding a current view while staying open | Sharp views held with appropriate uncertainty; reflection demonstrates real epistemic engagement |

**Composite scoring:**
- 17 to 20: Mastery. You've built a real practice for staying oriented as AI evolves.
- 13 to 16: Solid. The framework is in place, with room to deepen on one or two dimensions.
- 9 to 12: Developing. Signals are mostly fine but the updating criteria need work; revisit Lesson 4.
- Below 9: Restart. Read through the module again and try the activity fresh.
