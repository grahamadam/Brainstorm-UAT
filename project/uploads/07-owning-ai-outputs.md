## Module brief

This module is the closer, and it's intentionally about accountability rather than capability. Everything in the previous six modules has built toward this. You now know what AI is, where it came from, where it's going, how to prompt it, how to verify its output, and how to choose between tools. The remaining question is: who owns the work that AI helped you produce?

The answer is simple: you do. Always. Without exception.

This module is the **Diligence** competency from the 4Ds framework: taking responsibility for what you do with AI and how you do it. Diligence is the discipline that holds the other three competencies together. You can be excellent at delegation, description, and discernment, and still produce work that's harmful, irresponsible, or wrong if you don't own it. This module is about building the habits, rituals, and personal commitments that make ownership real, not theoretical.

It's also about closing AI 101 in a way that gives you something to carry forward. The most lasting outcome from this course shouldn't be a set of facts you remember. It should be a set of practices you actually use. We'll spend the last lesson translating everything you've learned into a personal AI policy you can live with.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

1. Articulate the accountability principle and what it means in practice for your day-to-day work.
2. Run a pre-publish ritual on any AI-assisted work before it goes out under your name.
3. Recognize what kinds of data are appropriate to put into which kinds of AI tools, and where the lines are.
4. Build a personal AI policy that codifies how you'll use AI going forward.

## Lesson 1: The accountability principle

The simplest way to state the principle is the one Brainstorm uses across the program: **AI is a draft, never a decision.** When you ship work that AI helped you produce, what you're shipping is *your* work. Your name is on it. Your judgment chose to send it out. Your reputation lives or dies by it. The AI doesn't share that accountability with you, because the AI doesn't have a name, a job, or a stake in the outcome.

This sounds obvious. In practice it's the principle people violate most often, usually without noticing. The pattern looks like this: someone uses AI to draft an email, glances at it, sends it. The email contains a small inaccuracy. A customer notices. The person says, "Sorry, the AI drafted that." They might say it as a joke, or as a deflection, or as an honest explanation. In every case, the framing is wrong.

The AI didn't send the email. The person did. The accountability was always with the person.

The L2 stance on this is unequivocal. You don't blame the tool when something goes wrong. You don't disclaim responsibility for AI-assisted work. You don't ship things you wouldn't be willing to defend on your own credibility. The AI is a tool you're using; the work is yours.

A useful test: imagine that everything AI helped you produce has your name printed at the top in 24-point font, and that you'll be asked to defend each specific claim if questioned. If that's a reasonable bar for the work you're sending out, you're operating at L2. If you'd flinch at that level of accountability for some of what you've sent recently, that's exactly where the work is.

### Why this matters more as capability grows

Here's the part that often gets missed. As AI gets more capable, the accountability problem gets *harder*, not easier.

When AI was clearly limited, people naturally treated its output as a starting point. They edited, verified, and rewrote, because they had to. As AI gets better, the temptation rises to ship what it produces with less and less of your own thought layered on top. That's the trap. The polished output looks finished. It feels finished. It often *is* finished, in a literal sense. But polished isn't the same as yours. Polish without ownership is how AI-assisted work goes off the rails at scale.

The L2 user inverts the instinct. The better the AI gets, the more deliberate your ownership has to be. Not because you're suspicious of the model. Because you're committed to the work being yours.

> **Try this (3 minutes).** Think about something you've sent or shipped in the past week that AI helped with. Just one piece. Now imagine you're being asked, point by point: what did you write yourself, what came from AI, what did you verify, what did you take on faith? Not as a gotcha. Just as a working professional describing your process. How comfortable are you with your answers? The discomfort, if there is any, is information about where your habits need to grow.

## Lesson 2: The pre-publish ritual

In M05 you learned a verification practice specifically focused on hallucinations. The pre-publish ritual is broader. It's the full check before AI-assisted work goes out under your name, and it includes verification but adds the ownership and judgment dimensions.

Here's a working version of the ritual. It takes a few minutes for most pieces of work.

**1. Did I add my own thinking, or am I shipping the AI's first draft?**

This is the ownership question. If the only thing distinguishing what you're sending from what the AI produced is "I read it once and didn't change anything," that's a flag. AI-assisted work should reflect *your* judgment, not just the model's output. What did you change? What did you keep? Why?

It's fine for the bones of a piece to come from AI. The voice, the judgment calls, and the specific decisions about what to include or exclude should be yours.

**2. Have I verified the load-bearing claims?**

This is M05's verification practice. Identify what would matter if it were wrong, check what's checkable, cut or soften what isn't. Specifically watch for: numbers, dates, names, citations, quotes, and anything time-sensitive.

**3. Does the tone match the relationship and the audience?**

AI defaults can be off-tone for your specific context. Too formal, too casual, too American, too generic. Read it as if you're the recipient. Does it sound like you would actually say this, to this person, in this context? If not, edit until it does.

**4. Am I shipping anything I wouldn't say in my own voice?**

Sometimes AI output includes a turn of phrase you wouldn't naturally use. A joke that doesn't sound like you. A claim more confident than your actual view. A flourish that's cleaner than your normal writing. These small mismatches accumulate; over time they erode the consistency of your professional voice. Edit them out. Your voice is part of your value.

**5. Would I be comfortable having this work attributed to me, with the AI assistance entirely invisible?**

This is the final test. If the answer is yes, ship it. If the answer is "well, mostly..." then there's something specific to fix.

### Making it a reflex, not a checklist

Reading the ritual takes longer than running it. After a few weeks of deliberate use, the questions collapse into a single quick instinct: *would I defend this as mine?* That instinct is the L2 standard. It runs in seconds, not minutes, and it catches the things that matter.

The discipline is in the early reps. Build the habit deliberately for a few weeks. After that, it becomes how you work.

> **Try this (3 minutes).** Take the most recent AI-assisted piece of work you produced. Run it through the five questions above. Be honest about each one. You don't need to redo the work. Just notice which questions you answered confidently and which ones gave you pause. The pauses are where your habits need attention.

## Lesson 3: Data handling and the boundaries

The other side of accountability is what you put *into* AI tools. Pasting things into a prompt is, in many cases, equivalent to putting that information into a third-party system. Treating that casually is one of the highest-risk L1 mistakes.

The full data governance picture is too big for a 101 course, and it varies by tool, by company, and by region. But here are the principles every L2 user needs to internalize.

### Different tools have different data policies

When you put data into an AI tool, what happens to it depends on the tool. Some tools train their models on user inputs by default. Some don't. Some retain conversation data; some don't. Some are configured for enterprise use with specific data protections; some are consumer products with different rules.

For Brainstorm and Gong-approved tools, this has been thought through carefully. There are policies in place about what data can go where, and the tools you have access to inside Gong are configured for safe use. You can put more in those tools than you can put in personal AI accounts.

For personal AI accounts on consumer tools, the rules are different. Treat anything you put into a personal ChatGPT, Claude.ai, or Gemini account the way you'd treat anything you'd post on a public forum. Maybe that's overkill in many cases, but it's the right safety margin, because the actual data handling depends on settings that may have changed since you last checked.

### The categories of information that need extra care

A few categories of information warrant extra caution regardless of tool:

**Customer-identifying information.** Names, contact info, deal-specific details, contract terms, anything that identifies a specific Gong customer. This is what makes Gong-approved tools the right choice. Putting customer details into a consumer AI account, even casually, is risky.

**Confidential internal information.** Strategy documents, financial data, unreleased product information, internal personnel details. The stuff that's clearly marked confidential, plus a lot of stuff that isn't formally marked but obviously is.

**Legal or compliance-sensitive content.** Anything that could be discoverable in litigation, anything covered by specific regulatory frameworks, anything where the chain of custody matters.

**Data covered by specific contractual obligations.** Customer data covered by your own agreements. Partner data covered by NDAs. Anything where you've made a specific commitment about how it will be handled.

**Personal data covered by privacy regulations.** GDPR, CCPA, and similar regulations apply to AI tools just like they apply to other systems.

When in doubt, ask before pasting. A two-minute check with security or legal beats a much bigger conversation about a leak after the fact.

### The reflex to build

The L2 reflex on data is: *what tool am I using, what's its data policy, and is what I'm about to paste in appropriate for that policy?* Three quick questions. They take seconds. They prevent the kind of mistake that's hard to come back from.

If you don't know your tool's data policy, that's the first homework. Take five minutes to look it up. Bookmark the page. The policy may shift over time, but knowing where it is gets you 80% of the way there.

> **Try this (3 minutes).** For each AI tool you use regularly, can you answer these questions: Does my company allow this tool? Is it approved for sensitive data? Where does my conversation data go? If you're not sure on any of them, take note. You don't have to fix it right now. But the questions belong to L2, and the answers should be ones you can produce confidently.

## Lesson 4: Building your personal AI policy

You've now covered everything in AI 101. The last thing to do is convert the course into something you'll actually use: a personal AI policy.

A personal AI policy is a short document, written by you for you, that captures how you'll use AI going forward. It covers the things that should be reflexive but only become reflexive if you've thought them through deliberately at least once. The act of writing it is the value, more than the document itself.

A useful structure for a personal AI policy includes:

**1. What I'll use AI for.** The categories of work where AI assistance fits well in your role. Prompted by M03 and M06.

**2. What I won't use AI for.** The categories where you've decided AI isn't the right tool. Maybe because the stakes are too high, the verification cost is too great, the data is too sensitive, or the work is yours specifically. Drawing a line here is more important than people realize.

**3. What verification I'll always run.** Your version of the pre-publish ritual. Specific to your work, but informed by M05 and this module.

**4. What data I won't put into which tools.** Your own data-handling rules, informed by Lesson 3 and the policies of your specific tools.

**5. How I'll disclose AI use.** When you'll mention that AI was involved (e.g., on certain kinds of work, in certain contexts) and when you won't (e.g., on routine work where it'd be noise to mention). There's no universal answer to this; it depends on your role, your audience, and your norms.

**6. What I'll keep learning.** A short note on what you'll do to stay sharp as the technology evolves. Brainstorm itself is part of this for you, since AI 201 and beyond are coming. But also: the signals you'll watch from M03, the prompts you'll keep refining from M04, the tools you'll experiment with from M06.

This isn't a binding policy. It's a working agreement with yourself. You'll revise it as your understanding deepens and as the tools change. The point isn't to write the perfect version. It's to make your default behavior intentional.

### One more thing: the disclosure question

Disclosure deserves a brief note, because it's the place where reasonable people most disagree, and there's no universal right answer.

In some contexts, disclosing AI use is clearly the right move. Academic work. Some kinds of journalism. Anywhere where the value of the work depends on it being demonstrably yours.

In other contexts, disclosing every AI use would be noisy and weird. Nobody mentions when they used spell-check. Nobody flags when they used a calendar tool to find a time. As AI integrates into more of the workflow, the case for routine disclosure of every use weakens.

The middle ground is judgment. Disclose when the level of AI involvement might matter to the recipient. Disclose when it'd be misleading to imply otherwise. Don't disclose every routine use that doesn't change the meaning of the work.

The principle that holds across the spectrum is honesty: never imply that work is more "yours" than it is in a way that misleads someone making a decision based on that. Beyond that, your specific disclosure norms are something to build into your personal policy.

> **Try this (5 minutes).** Sketch a one-paragraph version of each of the six sections above. Don't worry about getting it polished. Just get an initial pass written. The activity at the end of this module is the full version; this is the warmup.

## Closing thoughts

You've made it through AI 101. If you've genuinely engaged with the material, you've done the L1 → L2 transition. You're not a novice anymore. You can reach for AI in your work and use it well. You know what it is and isn't. You can write a prompt that gets useful output most of the time. You can recognize when an output is wrong, and you have habits for catching it before it reaches anyone else. You know what to put into which tools, and you know that what you ship is yours.

That's the foundation. AI 201 starts applying these foundations specifically to GTM work, and 301 gets role-specific. Each course builds on this one. The skills you've developed here will compound.

A few principles to carry forward:

**You own the work.** Always. The polish of the AI output doesn't transfer ownership; it just makes ownership feel less necessary. Stay deliberate about it.

**Iterate.** Your first prompt is a hypothesis. Your first reading of the output is a hypothesis. The work is in the second pass, and the third, and the fourth.

**Trust nothing on first read.** Verify what's checkable. Cut or soften what isn't. The cleaner the output, the more disciplined you should be.

**Skill compounds across tools.** The next great AI tool will arrive. The prompt-writing, verification, and ownership skills you've built will transfer. Build the skill; the tool is downstream.

**Stay curious, stay calibrated.** The technology is going to keep changing. Your job is not to predict where it goes. Your job is to stay oriented and update at the right pace. Watch your signals. Hold your views with appropriate uncertainty.

The L2 you is the one who shows up tomorrow with these habits. Welcome.

## Applied Activity: "Your Personal AI Policy"

**Estimated time: 30 to 45 minutes**
**Submission: a personal AI policy document + a short reflection**
**Graded against the rubric below**

### What you'll do

Write your personal AI policy. This is the capstone activity for AI 101, and it ties everything in the course together into something practical you'll actually use.

The policy should be a 1-page document, written by you for you, that captures how you intend to use AI going forward in your work. It's not a binding contract; it's a working agreement with yourself. The act of writing it is the value, more than the document itself.

### Structure

Your policy should have six short sections, each a paragraph or a small set of bullets:

**1. What I'll use AI for.**
The categories of work where AI assistance fits well in your role. Be specific. "Drafting emails" is okay; "first drafts of follow-up emails after customer calls" is better. List 4-7 specific uses.

**2. What I won't use AI for.**
Categories where you've decided AI isn't the right tool, for now or at all. This list is just as important as the first one. It might include high-stakes work where verification cost outweighs benefit, work where the value depends on it being demonstrably yours, or specific kinds of judgment calls.

**3. What verification I'll always run.**
Your version of the pre-publish ritual. Tailor it to your work. "I'll always verify any number, date, or citation" or "I'll always re-read for tone before sending to a customer" or whatever specifics matter for what you do.

**4. What data I won't put into which tools.**
Your own data-handling rules. Should reference at minimum: which tools you treat as enterprise-safe, which you treat as consumer-cautious, and what categories of content you won't paste in either way.

**5. How I'll handle disclosure.**
When and how you'll mention AI involvement in work you ship. It's fine if your answer is context-dependent; just be specific about the contexts.

**6. What I'll keep learning.**
A short note on how you'll stay sharp as the technology evolves. Reference at least one specific signal you'll watch (from M03), one specific habit you'll keep building (from any module), and what's next for you in Brainstorm.

### After the policy

Add a short reflection (about 150 words) answering: **Which section was hardest to write, and what does that tell you?** The honest answer often surfaces where your thinking is still fuzzy, which is the most useful information you'll get from this exercise.

### Submission

Submit a single document containing the six-section policy and the reflection. Don't over-design it. Plain text is fine. The point is that it's *your* policy, in *your* words, that you'll actually refer back to.

### Rubric

You'll be graded on four dimensions, each on a 1 to 5 scale, for a composite out of 20.

| Dimension | 1: Minimal | 3: Solid | 5: Excellent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Specificity** | Sections are vague ("I'll use AI for work things") and don't translate into actual day-to-day decisions | Sections are concrete enough to guide real decisions; the policy could be referred back to and used | Each section is specific, tailored to the writer's actual work, and clearly the result of real thinking, not just filling in a template |
| **Coverage of all six sections** | Two or more sections are weak or missing | All six sections are present and substantive | All six sections are present, well-developed, and integrated; the policy reads as a coherent whole rather than six disconnected lists |
| **Honest engagement** | Sections read like what someone is "supposed to" say; no real friction or limit-setting | At least one section shows the writer making a hard call, drawing a line, or admitting something they're still figuring out | Multiple sections show genuine engagement with trade-offs; the policy is shaped by the writer's actual situation, not by best-practice generalities |
| **Quality of reflection** | Generic; surface-level engagement with the question of what was hard | Identifies a specific section that was hard and articulates why | Reflection shows real meta-awareness; the difficulty surfaced reveals something useful about the writer's current thinking that they can grow from |

**Composite scoring:**
- 17 to 20: Mastery. The L1 → L2 transition is fully landed. You have a working policy and the meta-awareness to keep refining it.
- 13 to 16: Solid. The policy is real and useful, with room to deepen on specificity or honesty.
- 9 to 12: Developing. The exercise was completed but the policy is too generic to actually guide your work. Try again with more specificity to your role.
- Below 9: Restart. Revisit the module and write the policy fresh, with more thought to your specific context.
