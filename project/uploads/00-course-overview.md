# AI 101: Course Overview

**Brainstorm · AI Foundation · Universal track**
**Level transition: L1 (The Risk) → L2 (The Novice)**

## Why this course exists

If you work at Gong in a GTM role, you almost certainly use AI in some form already. You might draft an email with it, summarize a call, brainstorm a follow-up, or get a second opinion on a deal strategy. What's less certain is whether you're using it *well*. Most people aren't, and that's not a criticism. There hasn't really been a clear, structured way to learn the basics until very recently.

AI 101 is that structured way. It's the universal foundation every Gongster goes through before specializing. Think of it as the on-ramp. By the time you finish, you'll have a working mental model of what AI actually is, you'll be able to write a prompt that gets you what you actually want most of the time, you'll know how to spot when a model is wrong, and you'll have a clear sense of what *you* are accountable for when you use AI in your work.

That's it. AI 101 doesn't try to make you an expert. It tries to make you safe and capable. The deeper specialization (how to use AI in your specific role, how to chain tools together, how to lead AI adoption) comes in 201 and beyond.

## Where this sits in Brainstorm

Brainstorm uses a five-level fluency progression to describe where any GTM employee sits today and how they grow over time:

| Level | Name | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| L1 | **The Risk** | Uses unvetted tools without a security or accuracy mindset |
| L2 | **The Novice** | Can write a prompt and use AI for simple tasks, but mostly as a "user" |
| L3 | **The Practitioner** | Co-creates with AI, fact-checks outputs, and iterates |
| L4 | **The Strategist** | Orchestrates multi-step workflows across tools |
| L5 | **The Architect** | Builds new systems, coaches peers, leads transformation |

**AI 101 moves you from L1 to L2.** That sounds modest, and it is, by design. The honest truth is that most of the GTM org sits somewhere between L1 and L2 today. Getting solidly to L2 is the single most valuable shift you can make, because it eliminates the categories of mistakes that hurt customers and waste time. Every module in this course is designed around making that transition real and durable.

## The framework you'll keep coming back to

Throughout AI 101 you'll see a four-part framework called the **4Ds**: **Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence.** It's not a Gong invention. It was developed by Professors Rick Dakan (Ringling College of Art and Design) and Joseph Feller (University College Cork) in collaboration with Anthropic, and it's used widely as the standard way to describe what fluency with AI actually consists of.

Here's the short version:

- **Delegation.** Deciding what to do with AI versus what to do yourself.
- **Description.** Communicating clearly with AI so it understands what you actually want.
- **Discernment.** Evaluating what the AI gives back with a critical eye.
- **Diligence.** Taking responsibility for what you produce with AI.

Each of these maps to a different part of how a real interaction with AI unfolds, and each has its own skills you can develop. AI 101 introduces all four, with most of the depth landing on Description (how to prompt) and Discernment (how to evaluate). Delegation and Diligence get foundation here and go deeper in 201+.

The 4Ds are licensed under Creative Commons (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0), which is why we can build on them directly. Full attribution is at the end of every module that uses them.

## The seven modules

AI 101 is structured in two halves: literacy first (build the mental model), then craft (develop the actual skill). Each module is roughly 30 minutes of content plus a 10 to 15 minute knowledge check and an applied activity.

### Literacy: Build the mental model

**M01 · What is AI?**
Define what AI is and isn't. Demystify the vocabulary you'll keep hearing: model, LLM, prompt, agent, token, context. Understand why data is the foundation of everything.

**M02 · How did we get here?**
The arc from rule-based systems to machine learning to large language models. Why the moment we're in is genuinely different from previous "AI" hype cycles, and what changed to make it different.

**M03 · Where is this going?**
The trajectory over the next two to three years. What's worth preparing for, what's hype to ignore, and how to keep your bearings as the technology evolves underneath you.

### Fluency: Develop the craft

**M04 · Prompting best practices**
How to communicate with AI so it actually delivers what you want. Context, role, task, format, examples. The difference between a one-line prompt and one that gets you a usable first draft.

**M05 · Avoiding hallucinations**
Why models confidently make things up, when it's most likely to happen, and the practical habits that catch it before it reaches a customer or a teammate.

**M06 · Right tool for the job**
Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT, Gong AI. What each is good at, when to reach for which, and how to think about choosing without getting paralyzed.

**M07 · Owning AI outputs**
You are accountable for what you ship. AI is a draft, never a decision. The mindset, habits, and small rituals that keep you in control of your own work.

## How to take this course

A few things will make a real difference in how much you actually get out of this.

**Treat the embedded exercises as required, not optional.** Every module has small "try it" prompts woven into the lessons. They take a couple of minutes each, and they're the difference between reading about AI and actually getting better at using it. Skip them and you'll pass the knowledge check. Do them and you'll change how you work.

**Use a real AI tool while you go.** You'll get the most out of this if you have Claude.ai (or whichever AI tool you have access to) open in another tab. The exercises assume you'll actually try things, not just imagine trying them.

**Don't rush the activity.** Each module ends with an applied activity, which is a small piece of real work you do with AI and submit. These are graded against a rubric, and the rubric is the most direct signal of whether the concepts are sticking. If you're not sure about a piece of feedback, that's a signal to revisit the module section it relates to.

**Iterate.** This shows up later in the course, but it's worth saying up front: your first prompt is rarely your best prompt. Anthropic's [research on AI fluency](https://www.anthropic.com/research/AI-fluency-index) found that the single strongest predictor of every other fluency behavior is whether someone iterates and refines in conversation rather than accepting the first thing the model gives them. Bring that mindset to the exercises.

## What "done" looks like

By the end of AI 101, you should be able to:

1. Explain in plain language what an LLM is, what it does, and what it doesn't do.
2. Place the current AI moment in honest context, neither overhyped nor underestimated.
3. Write a prompt that gives the model enough to work with on the first try.
4. Recognize when a model output is likely wrong, and know what to do about it.
5. Choose between the major AI tools available to you with a real reason.
6. Describe what you, as the human, are responsible for when you ship work that AI helped you produce.

If those six things become second nature, you've made the L1 → L2 jump. From there, AI 201 starts applying these foundations to GTM work specifically, and 301 gets role-specific.
